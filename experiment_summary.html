This document provides a detailed summary of the experimental journey undertaken in the Lunar Lander Offline Reinforcement Learning project. Experimental Phases and Approaches

Phase 1: Initial CQL Baseline Evaluation

The project began by establishing a foundational understanding of how a value-based Offline RL agent, specifically Conservative Q-Learning (CQL), performs when trained on demonstration data of varying quality. Three distinct datasets—Expert, Medium, and Random—were generated, each containing 50,000 transitions, to simulate different levels of agent expertise. These datasets were created using PPO from `stable_baselines3`. The CQL models, based on `DiscreteCQLConfig` with an MLP architecture, were trained for 30,000 gradient steps. This phase aimed to quantify CQL's robustness and learning capabilities across this spectrum of data.

Phase 2: Decision Transformer (DT) Integration and Initial Comparison

Building upon the CQL baseline, the Decision Transformer (DT) algorithm was introduced. DT, a sequence-modeling approach to Offline RL, offered a different paradigm for learning from demonstrations. This phase aimed to conduct a direct comparative analysis between DT and CQL. To meet DT's data requirements, the size of all datasets was significantly increased to 500,000 transitions. The DT models utilized a `context_size` of 20 and initial evaluations were conducted with a fixed high `target_return` of 200.0, which served to highlight its sensitivity to the quality of the provided target. Training of DT models was performed on a GPU, leveraging CUDA for accelerated computation.

Phase 3: Refined Medium Data and Dynamic DT Target Returns

A critical insight from Phase 2 was the significant impact of the target return on DT's performance, especially with sub-optimal data. This phase focused on refining the dataset generation, particularly for the "Medium" dataset. This "True Medium" dataset was created by training a PPO agent until its rolling average reward reached exactly 20.0, ensuring a precisely controlled intermediate level of expertise. Most importantly, DT evaluations were adjusted to use dynamic target returns, aligning the requested return with what was realistically achievable from each dataset's quality: 200.0 for Expert, 40.0 for Medium, and -100.0 for Random datasets. This change provided a more accurate and fair assessment of DT's capabilities across different data qualities.

Phase 4: Optimized Decision Transformer Performance

The final experimental phase was dedicated to optimizing the Decision Transformer's performance through targeted hyperparameter tuning and the application of established best practices. Key enhancements included expanding the DT's `context_size` from 20 to 50, allowing the model to process a longer history of interactions. Additionally, `reward_scaler="standard"` was applied to normalize rewards, stabilizing training. Further fine-tuning involved adjusting `batch_size` to 64 and `learning_rate` to 1e-4. These optimizations aimed to maximize the DT's learning efficiency and overall performance, resulting in the most robust metrics for the algorithm.

Phase 5: Intermediate Data Level Evaluation

To bridge the gap between "Expert" and "Medium" data, a fourth data quality level—Intermediate—was introduced. A PPO agent was trained until its rolling average reward reached exactly 100.0, capturing a proficiency level where the agent consistently survives but often misses the landing pad. This dataset provided the Decision Transformer with its first encounter of successful "scripts" buried within sub-optimal noise. DT was evaluated with a dynamic target_return of 150.0 to test its ability to exceed the teacher's average performance.
