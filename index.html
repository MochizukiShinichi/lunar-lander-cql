Lunar Lander Offline RL

Investigating the impact of demonstration data quality on Decision Transformer

Abstract

This experiment evaluates how the quality of demonstration data affects Offline Reinforcement Learning agents, comparing the MLP-based Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) and the Sequence-based Decision Transformer (DT) algorithms. Unlike standard Reinforcement Learning which learns by trial and error, Offline RL learns exclusively from a fixed dataset. We compared three data levels: Expert (fully trained), Medium (partially trained), and Random (untrained).

Conclusion

The results provide a clear demonstration of the architectural trade-offs between Decision Transformers (DT) and Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) across varying data qualities:

  • Peak Performance on Expert Data: The Decision Transformer achieved the highest overall score (243.6 with a 90% success rate), easily beating the CQL model. This highlights DT's strength in sequence modeling—when provided with excellent demonstrations, it can seamlessly mimic and reproduce optimal behavior.
  • Data-Bound Prediction Constraints: Conversely, DT's performance is strictly bounded by the maximum trajectory quality within its dataset. While the True Medium DT vastly improved to a score of -64.8 when correctly queried with a realistic target_return, the purely Random DT collapsed entirely (-481.5). Because DT predicts actions rather than values, it cannot easily "invent" better behaviors from noisy data.
  • Robustness of Q-Learning: CQL historically proves more robust when dealing with completely random or low-quality transitions. By leveraging temporal difference (TD) learning and dynamic programming, CQL can theoretically "stitch together" scattered good actions from sub-optimal data.

Ultimately, while Decision Transformers demonstrate an impressive ability to surpass classical algorithms when given abundant expert data, traditional offline RL algorithms like CQL remain the safer choice for highly noisy, mixed, or heavily sub-optimal datasets.

Appendix: Experiment Setup

1. Environment

The experiment was conducted using Gymnasium's LunarLander-v3. This is a classic "box2d" control task where the agent must land a craft on a designated pad using three engines (Main, Left, Right).

2. Dataset Generation

Three datasets were generated using Stable-Baselines3 (PPO) to simulate different levels of expertise:

  • Expert: PPO agent trained to convergence (500,000 steps, average reward > 200).
  • Medium: PPO agent trained strictly until its rolling average reward reached exactly 20.0, meticulously capturing true intermediate proficiency.
  • Random: Actions sampled uniformly from the discrete action space.

Each dataset contains exactly 50,000 transitions (state, action, reward, next state).

3. Training Details

The offline agents were trained using the Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) and Decision Transformer (DT) implementation from the d3rlpy library.

  • Steps: 30,000 gradient steps per model.
  • Architecture: DiscreteDecisionTransformer (context_size=20, max_timestep=1000) vs DiscreteCQLConfig (MLP default).
  • Hardware: Training performed on GPU (NVIDIA RTX 2080 via CUDA 12.1) to accelerate Transformer sequence modeling.

4. Evaluation Methodology

Each trained model was evaluated over 50 test episodes. Success was defined as a final cumulative reward ≥ 200. The simulation videos capture the unadulterated agent behavior exactly as rendered natively by the Gymnasium environment.

Q&A

Q: Why does the Decision Transformer perform so poorly on the Random dataset? Is it just undertrained?

A: No, it is not undertrained. The training logs confirm that the neural network fully converged. For instance, the cross-entropy loss for the random dataset quickly dropped to 1.24 and then completely flatlined for tens of thousands of steps. Because LunarLander has 4 discrete actions, a completely random baseline yields a cross-entropy loss of -log(0.25) ≈ 1.386. A loss of 1.24 indicates the model successfully learned whatever faint, predictable correlations existed in the random data, but hit a hard mathematical ceiling and converged to its maximum predictive capacity.

The total failure is actually a fundamental limitation of the algorithmic concept. Decision Transformers formulate RL as a sequence modeling task, meaning they condition their actions on a predefined "target return". If you ask DT to achieve a target return of 200.0 when the highest reward ever seen in a random dataset was -50, it is forced to extrapolate entirely out-of-distribution without any logical paths to pull from. However, when we queried it realistically (like dynamically assigning a target return of 40.0 to the True Medium model), the DT faithfully reconstructed decent trajectories and scored a massive improvement at -64.8!

Q: Why is CQL far more robust on sub-optimal data than DT?

A: CQL's robustness comes from two major architectural differences:

  1. Dynamic Programming ("Stitching"): Unlike DT, which only looks at whole intact sequences, CQL relies on the Bellman equation to learn the Temporal Difference (TD) value of individual state-to-state transitions. This allows CQL to "stitch" together one smart action from an otherwise terrible trajectory and another smart action from a different episode, allowing it to piece together a good overall policy from noisy data.
  2. Conservative Q-Penalties: Normal offline Q-learning networks tend to wildly overestimate the value of unseen actions, causing them to confidently fail. CQL mathematically penalizes the Q-values of actions outside the dataset distribution, forcing the agent to predictably stick to known safe paths. DT has no such mechanism to restrict dangerous extrapolation.
